There is a song called *"The Capital of Poland Is Shanghai"* that has become the unofficial anthem of an entire regional music scene. The band that wrote it never particularly set out to be famous — which, depending on how you look at it, is exactly how it should be.

**你男友係碌葛 yourboyfriendsucks!** (abbreviated YBS) are from Guangzhou, the port city at the mouth of the Pearl River Delta. They formed in 2010 because several friends happened to share a devotion to The Jesus and Mary Chain. They played gigs where the audience numbered five — mostly other bands. They released a debut album comprising the exact same song eleven times, each track title a different language's translation of "The Capital of Poland Is Shanghai." Critics called them amateurish. The band didn't much mind.

They are, by most accounts, one of the more significant indie bands to have emerged from China in the past two decades — which is worth sitting with for a moment, given everything that follows.

---

## Guangzhou Connection 

China's southern coastal belt — running roughly from Guangzhou up to Shanghai — has always had a character distinct from the north. The climate is subtropical. Historical ties to Hong Kong and Taiwan run deep. Languages form a mosaic: Cantonese, Hakka, Teochew, and dozens of others alongside Mandarin. Outside influences arrived here earlier and were absorbed more readily than in Beijing — a fact that shaped everything that followed.

By the 2000s, this had produced a small but energetic underground music scene. MP3 downloads were bringing overseas sounds in near real-time. Emo and post-hardcore bands were forming in garages and borrowed basements. And in Guangzhou, a loose circle of young rock enthusiasts — who had found each other through the social network Douban and through local gigs — coalesced under a name borrowed from a local condiment brand: 富力保 Full Label.

### Full Label, An amateur collective

Full Label was not, strictly speaking, a label. It was not much of an organisation either. It was a group of friends who went to gigs together, formed bands together, and occasionally played guerrilla sets in locations that included a university rooftop, a bookstore, a skateboard shop, a fast food forecourt, and at least one stranger's garden. They were sometimes removed within ten minutes. This, apparently, was part of the appeal.

### The Band Village

Around the same time, members of a local emo band  had discovered an abandoned bomb shelter beneath a residential complex — previously a rubbish dump — and converted it into a shared rehearsal space. This became known as the Band村 Band Village.

At its peak, nearly fifty bands used the space. It was self-governed, with elected committees, actual rent payments, and a set of rules that were taken seriously. One member, recounting its origins, cited a DIY arts space in Hong Kong as the inspiration. They called themselves villagers.

The Band Village lasted five years before fire regulations, triggered by a nearby building fire in 2012, forced its closure. In the years it was active, it functioned as rehearsal room, community centre, and the closest thing the scene had to a home base — often all three at once.

### yourboyfriendsucks! Assemble

YBS came together in 2010 from within the Full Label circle. The founding line-up was guitarists 小吉 Siugat and 史悲 Space, bassist 羅密欧 Romeo, and a succession of drummers. Siugat handled vocals initially.

Their sets opened — and would always open — with a cover of "Just Like Honey." The sound leant more towards trash and freak-rock than proper shoegaze, but something about that roughness made the pop instincts underneath — the JAMC inheritance — come through more clearly, not less. It worked in ways that were difficult to predict in advance.

In the summer of 2011, Zoey joined as lead vocalist, and the band found its voice. She brought a warmth and directness that became inseparable from what YBS sounded like. Her lyrics moved between English, German, Mandarin, and Cantonese — sometimes within a single song — which gives you some sense of the kind of band this was.

In 2012, YBS self-released their debut "album," *死 (Death)*: eleven tracks, one song, eleven different translations of the same title. Some listeners were baffled. Some were charmed. Reviews described the band as crude and amateurish. The band moved on.

### Everyone Loves Sarah Records

Whilst YBS were playing to five people in Guangzhou, something similar was taking shape in nearby Shenzhen. A collective called 無聊製造 Boring Productions had set up along much the same lines: book gigs, form bands, invite bands, repeat.

Its founder, Jovi, had developed a thorough obsession with Sarah Records — the Bristol indie label that released twee-pop and jangly guitar music from 1987 to 1995, then closed deliberately on its own terms. Jovi showed the Sarah Records documentary to anyone who would sit still long enough, and the enthusiasm spread readily. If you know anyone from this scene and want to understand where they're coming from, Sarah Records is a reasonable place to start.

The label became a kind of shared reference point across the southern scene. Full Label began making fanzines. A common sensibility — modest melodies, DIY principles, emotional directness, genuine indifference to commercial music — spread through Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Xiamen, and beyond. That a short-lived Bristol label from the late 1980s became the common language of a DIY scene in southern China is an odd fact, but there it is.

On the 20th anniversary of Sarah Records' dissolution, in August 2015, Boring Productions and Full Label jointly released a tribute compilation, Our Secret World. Boring Productions pressed the CD; Full Label handled the cassette. The tracklist included Golden Cage's Hover, Chinese Football's 徐波 XuBo, and various other figures from the southern scene.

The Sarah Records founder later sent Boring Productions a personal message of gratitude. This reportedly moved several people to tears, which feels entirely appropriate.

### Qiii Snacks Records

Shortly after Our Secret World, Full Label quietly wound down. 小赵Xiao Zhao, its founding organiser stepped back from the music world — he now runs an audio equipment rental business, apparently.

Siugat and the remaining members regrouped and, in 2015, formalised as 琪琪音像 Qiii Snacks Records. The name comes from a local snack shop the members used to frequent — 祺祺小食 — combined with the word for "video shop" (音像), a nod to the nostalgia of childhood media rental. The logo is a bottle cap.

Qiii Snacks was essentially Full Label with marginally more structure — still entirely amateur (everyone has a day job), still resolutely DIY, but now operating recognisably as a record label. The stated motto translates roughly as "rough output over no output," which tells you most of what you need to know about the philosophy. According to Siugat, the finances run about even — no profit, no loss. The label's most-quoted line is that its most reliable income is the monthly interest on a savings account: approximately twenty yuan, or about three dollars. They seem perfectly fine with this.

The label's first significant project was bringing Taiwanese emo act 透明雜誌 Touming Magazine on a four-city mainland tour — which is, by some accounts, the reason Qiii Snacks was formalised at all. Members pooled money, arranged accommodation, booked venues, and sorted the logistics themselves. The tour turned a modest profit. That profit funded the next thing.

In May 2016, YBS released their first proper studio EP, *第一集 (Volume One)*, through Qiii Snacks — six songs, with cover artwork by Space, whose cartoon illustrations would later appear on releases by Chinese Football as well. Shortly after the EP came out, Zoey left for Germany to study, and the band went on hiatus.

The EP found its way around. Streaming platforms helped. People who had never heard of Guangzhou's underground scene found "The Capital of Poland Is Shanghai" and clearly felt something. By the time YBS briefly reunited in 2019 — to considerably larger audiences than anything from their original run — the song had become something of an anthem for a generation of southern Chinese indie listeners. The band hadn't changed. The rest of the world had simply caught up.

### The Hub

Qiii Snacks has since grown into something that functions, in practice, as a hub for DIY indie across China and beyond. Their catalogue includes releases from artists in Hong Kong, Taiwan, Singapore, and Tokyo. They have worked with labels in Beijing (Genjin Records, Nugget Records), Wuhan (Wild Records), and Hong Kong (Sweaty & Cramped).

The influence has spread in other directions too. Nick, then a member of Shanghai shoegaze outfit Soft, attended an Express tour show in Guangzhou, saw what Qiii Snacks was doing, went home, and started his own label — 生煎唱片 SJ Records. It's a pattern that repeats itself across the scene: someone encounters something that feels right, and goes away wanting to do it themselves.

There is also a physical shop in Guangzhou called Portal, selling records and merchandise, with occasional small events. It reportedly has several resident cats.

### The Final Show

YBS reunited in 2023 for a nine-city farewell tour with guest bands, closing at home in Guangzhou. At that final show, they announced their dissolution. Siugat ended the night by recalling something Xiao Zhao — the man who had started Full Label — used to say: "What we're trying to do is make music that warms people's hearts." Which, in the end, they did.

Their recorded output, in total, is modest: a debut "album" conceived as a joke, two EPs, a handful of live recordings. The music has outlasted all of that by quite some distance. What YBS and the Qiii Snacks circle built wasn't really a career in any conventional sense. It was more like a way of doing things — and it turned out to matter to a lot of people. The music is still there.
