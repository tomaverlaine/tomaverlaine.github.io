## Introduction

Active since 2008 under the banner of post-punk and no wave, 疯医 The Fallacy are a band like no other. Frontman Wang Xubo has never been shy about his devotion to the legendary Australian post-punk outfit The Birthday Party, and the band’s explosive, convulsive style carries that legacy forward. Combined with their freak-out saxophone work, they stand apart even within the Chinese rock scene.

According to Wang Xubo, what drew him to The Birthday Party is encapsulated in Nick Cave’s description of the band’s guitarist — a completely unique sound defined by sudden bursts of explosive noise, at once chaotic and strangely beautiful, something they had never heard before.

They have earned a loyal following among rock fans and are regulars at major music festivals. Personally, I would go so far as to call them the coolest rock band in the world — and at the very least, one of the best bands in Asia.

## Beyond the Centre: Xinxiang

Nowadays it is not unusual for bands outside Beijing to operate on a national scale, though most are based in Shanghai, Guangzhou, or other major hubs. The Fallacy are a rare exception.

Hailing from Xinxiang, a modest city in Henan Province far from the music industry’s centre of gravity, they are often regarded with curiosity. Wang Xubo’s day job as a newspaper journalist is often mentioned in the same breath.

In recent years, a remarkable number of bands have emerged from Henan, with Xinxiang as a point of origin. This piece turns its attention to that local scene, with The Fallacy at its centre.

## “Rock City”

On April 11, 1998, a rock festival billed as the “China New Music Concert” was held at Xinxiang Sports Center stadium. Featuring leading figures such as Tang Dynasty, the event drew 30,000 fans — an unprecedented gathering at the time.

Rock, long suppressed throughout much of the 1990s, was stepping back into the light. A second festival followed the next year, and the event later came to be described as “China’s Woodstock”. Xinxiang began to be spoken of as a holy site of Chinese rock, earning the nickname “Rock City”.

The impact was not merely symbolic. With limited access to the internet and few entertainment options, live music began appearing everywhere — in public squares, shopping malls, and even at noodle shop openings. Kids formed bands as casually as others went to karaoke.

Even today, the density of music stores and lesson studios reflects that legacy. In Xinxiang, rock is not exotic — it is part of everyday life.

## A Scene of Contradictions

Despite the mythology, local musicians often have mixed feelings about the “Rock City” label. The scene itself remains small, and rock has never truly become mainstream.

Yet the cost of living is low, and the pressure to “make it” is minimal. For those not seeking commercial success, Xinxiang offers something rare: a sustainable environment in which to simply play music.

## The Formation of The Fallacy

Wang Xubo was among those whose lives were changed by the 1998 concert. As a teenager, his fascination with rock began with Tang Dynasty, before expanding toward New York’s underground through magazines and CD supplements.

Without the internet, rock culture circulated through print — often driven more by idealism than commerce. It was likely during this period that he encountered The Birthday Party, alongside the literary sensibilities that would later define his work.

In 2007, Wang met bassist Zhang Nan, and together with drummer Sun Zhaowei formed the band. The English name The Fallacy was chosen in place of a literal translation of “疯医”, which they felt sounded too clumsy.

## Expansion and Recognition

In 2008, drummer Yang Baobin — formerly of P.K.14 — joined the band. His connections likely helped secure an early support slot for P.K.14 in Zhengzhou, marking a turning point.

Impressed by their originality, Yang Haisong recommended them to the Beijing-based label Modern Sky.

In 2010, their track appeared on a compilation, followed by a contract and debut album in 2011. They became the first band from Henan to sign with the label.

## Local Infrastructure

Parallel to the band’s rise, a local ecosystem was forming.

Xu Xiaodong opened Ark Livehouse in 2010, later relocating and expanding it into Neverland — a venue that remains central to the scene. His role extended far beyond that of a promoter; he actively sustained the community, often at personal financial cost.

Events such as student festivals further reinforced the continuity of the scene, introducing new generations to rock culture.

## Parallel Bands and Shared Networks

Xinxiang’s scene is defined by overlap. Musicians frequently play in multiple bands, forming a tightly interconnected network.

Pumpkins, alongside The Fallacy, represent one of the scene’s key pillars, leaning more toward punk rock. Members move fluidly between projects, reflecting both necessity and community cohesion.

## Evolution of Sound

Across their discography, The Fallacy have undergone gradual but significant changes.

- Early work emphasized raw, documentary-style recording
- Later releases incorporated more deliberate production
- Lyrics shifted from English to Chinese
- Literary references deepened, drawing from Baudelaire and Yeats

The addition of saxophonist Li Zenghui in 2020 expanded their sonic palette, integrating elements of free jazz and experimental music.

## Maturity and Synthesis

Their 2023 self-titled album represents a culmination.

The saxophone becomes structural rather than ornamental, reshaping the band’s internal dynamics. Post-punk remains the foundation, but noise and improvisation are fully integrated.

The closing track, “Neverland”, functions as a retrospective gesture, referencing their entire catalogue while pointing back to the venue that nurtured them.

## The Present and Beyond

The band now operates as a five-piece, continuing to evolve.

Meanwhile, new initiatives such as CCLIVE — a grassroots streaming project — suggest that Xinxiang’s scene is not static but expanding outward.

Emerging bands continue to appear, and new support structures are being proposed.

## Conclusion

The story of The Fallacy is inseparable from the city that shaped them.

Xinxiang is not a “rock city” in the sense of scale or industry presence. Rather, it is a place where rock persists — embedded in everyday life, sustained by individuals, and passed quietly from one generation to the next.

Fifteen years on, The Fallacy remain what they have always been: children who fell in love with rock music, and never left.
