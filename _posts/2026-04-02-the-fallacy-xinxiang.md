## Introduction

Active since 2008 under the banner of post-punk and no wave, The Fallacy are a band like no other. Frontman Wang Xubo has never been shy about his devotion to the legendary Australian post-punk outfit The Birthday Party — a band active in the late 1970s and early 1980s and widely regarded as a precursor to noise rock and no wave aesthetics — and the band’s explosive, convulsive style carries that legacy forward. Combined with their freak-out saxophone work, they stand apart even within the Chinese rock scene.

According to Wang Xubo, what drew him to The Birthday Party is encapsulated in Nick Cave’s description of the band’s guitarist — referring to Rowland S. Howard, whose playing was characterized by jagged, dissonant textures and sudden bursts of noise — as producing a completely unique sound: chaotic, violent, yet strangely beautiful.

They have earned a loyal following among rock fans and are regulars at major music festivals. Personally, I would go so far as to call them the coolest rock band in the world — and at the very least, one of the best bands in Asia.

## Beyond the Centre: Xinxiang

Nowadays it is not unusual for bands outside Beijing to operate on a national scale, though most are based in Shanghai, Guangzhou, or other major hubs of China’s music industry. The Fallacy are a rare exception.

Hailing from Xinxiang, a modest city in Henan Province in central China, far removed from the country’s primary cultural and commercial centres, they are often regarded with curiosity. Wang Xubo’s day job as a newspaper journalist — a relatively stable profession in a context where full-time musicianship remains economically precarious — is often mentioned in the same breath.

In recent years, a remarkable number of bands have emerged from Henan, with Xinxiang as a point of origin, forming what some observers have begun to describe as a loosely defined regional scene. This piece turns its attention to that local scene, with The Fallacy at its centre.

## “Rock City”

On April 11, 1998, a rock festival billed as the “China New Music Concert” was held at Xinxiang Sports Center stadium. Featuring leading figures such as Tang Dynasty — one of the most influential Chinese heavy metal bands of the early 1990s — the event drew 30,000 fans, an unprecedented gathering at the time.

Rock, which had been subject to varying degrees of suppression throughout much of the 1990s, was stepping back into the public sphere. A second festival followed the next year, and the event later came to be described as “China’s Woodstock”, a comparison that signals both its scale and its symbolic importance. Xinxiang began to be spoken of as a holy site of Chinese rock, earning the nickname “Rock City”.

The impact was not merely symbolic. With internet access still limited and entertainment options relatively scarce in late-1990s provincial China, live music began appearing everywhere — in public squares, shopping malls, and even at commercial events such as restaurant openings. Kids formed bands as casually as others went to karaoke, indicating a shift in how music was socially practiced.

Even today, the relatively high density of music stores and private lesson studios in the city reflects that legacy. In Xinxiang, rock is not exotic — it is embedded in everyday life.

## A Scene of Contradictions

Despite the mythology, local musicians often have mixed feelings about the “Rock City” label. The actual scale of the scene remains modest, and rock has never become a mainstream cultural form in the city.

Yet the cost of living is low, and the pressure to achieve commercial success is comparatively minimal. For those not seeking industry recognition, Xinxiang offers something rare: an environment in which music can be sustained as an ongoing practice rather than a career imperative.

## The Formation of The Fallacy

Wang Xubo was among those whose lives were changed by the 1998 concert. As a teenager, his fascination with rock began with Tang Dynasty before expanding toward New York’s underground — likely referring to scenes such as no wave and post-punk — through magazines and CD supplements that circulated widely in China in the pre-internet era.

At the time, rock culture was mediated largely through print publications, which often leaned toward romanticism and subcultural idealism rather than market logic. It was likely during this period that he encountered The Birthday Party, alongside the literary sensibilities that would later shape his lyric writing.

In 2007, Wang met bassist Zhang Nan, and together with drummer Sun Zhaowei formed the band. The English name The Fallacy was chosen in place of a literal translation of “Crazy Doctor” in Chinese, which they felt sounded awkward or overly literal in English.

## Expansion and Recognition

In 2008, drummer Yang Baobin — formerly of P.K.14, one of Beijing’s most influential post-punk bands — joined the group. His presence likely facilitated early connections beyond the local scene, including a support slot for P.K.14 in Zhengzhou, the provincial capital of Henan.

Yang Haisong, a central figure in the Beijing indie scene and a member of P.K.14, was impressed by the band and recommended them to Modern Sky, one of China’s most prominent independent labels.

In 2010, their track appeared on a label compilation, followed by a formal contract and debut album in 2011. They became the first band from Henan to sign with the Beijing-based company, marking a rare instance of regional breakthrough.

## Local Infrastructure

Parallel to the band’s rise, a local ecosystem was forming.

Xu Xiaodong opened Ark Livehouse in 2010 — widely regarded as Xinxiang’s first venue dedicated specifically to rock music — later relocating and expanding it into Neverland, a space capable of hosting several hundred people. His role extended far beyond that of a venue operator; he actively sustained the scene, often prioritizing accessibility over profitability.

Events such as student-oriented festivals further reinforced the continuity of the scene, functioning both as entry points for new audiences and as platforms for emerging bands.

## Parallel Bands and Shared Networks

Xinxiang’s scene is defined by overlap. Musicians frequently play in multiple bands, forming a tightly interconnected network — a pattern partly driven by the limited number of active participants.

Pumpkins, alongside The Fallacy, represent one of the scene’s central pillars, leaning more toward straightforward punk rock. Members move fluidly between projects, reflecting both practical necessity and a shared communal ethos.

## Evolution of Sound

Across their discography, The Fallacy have undergone gradual but significant changes.

Early work emphasized a raw, almost documentary recording style, while later releases incorporated more deliberate production. Lyrics gradually shifted from English to Chinese, suggesting a change in both intended audience and expressive strategy.

Literary references became more explicit, drawing on figures such as Charles Baudelaire — the 19th-century French poet associated with modernity and decadence — and W. B. Yeats, indicating a sustained engagement with translated literary traditions.

The addition of saxophonist Li Zenghui in 2020 — a musician active in Beijing’s experimental and avant-garde scenes — expanded their sonic palette, introducing elements of free jazz and improvisation into their established framework.

## Maturity and Synthesis

Their 2023 self-titled album represents a culmination.

Here, the saxophone functions not merely as an added texture but as a structural element, reshaping the band’s internal dynamics. While post-punk remains the foundation, noise, free jazz, and experimental approaches are fully integrated.

The closing track, “Neverland”, operates as a retrospective gesture, referencing their catalogue while also pointing to the venue that has supported them — reinforcing the reciprocal relationship between band and place.

## The Present and Beyond

The band now operates as a five-piece, continuing to evolve.

Meanwhile, new initiatives such as CCLIVE — a grassroots live-streaming project often compared to KEXP, the Seattle-based public radio platform known for its live sessions — suggest that Xinxiang’s scene is not static but gradually extending outward.

Emerging bands continue to appear, and new support structures are being proposed, indicating a slow but persistent expansion.

## Conclusion

The story of The Fallacy is inseparable from the city that shaped them.

Xinxiang is not a “rock city” in terms of industry scale or institutional presence. Rather, it is a place where rock persists — embedded in everyday life, sustained by individuals, and transmitted across generations through informal networks.

Fifteen years on, The Fallacy remain what they have always been: children who fell in love with rock music, and never left.
